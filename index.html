Introduction

Treaty 1 was signed on August 3, 1871, at Lower Fort Garry near present-day Winnipeg, Manitoba. It was one of the first numbered treaties made after Canada became a country in 1867.

The treaty was an agreement between the Canadian government and several First Nations including the Anishinaabe (Ojibway) and the Swampy Cree. The agreement aimed to create peaceful relationships while allowing settlers to move into the region.

Quick Facts About Treaty 1

  • Signed: August 3, 1871
  • Location: Lower Fort Garry, Manitoba
  • Nations involved: Anishinaabe (Ojibway) and Swampy Cree
  • One of the first numbered treaties in Canada

Signatories

Treaty 1 was signed by representatives of the Canadian government including Adams George Archibald and Wemyss M. Simpson. First Nations leaders representing the Anishinaabe and Swampy Cree peoples also participated in the negotiations.

These leaders discussed how land would be shared while ensuring Indigenous communities could continue hunting, fishing, and maintaining their cultural traditions.

Impact

Treaty 1 allowed the Canadian government to expand settlement into western Canada. Over time, towns, farms, and transportation routes developed across the region.

For First Nations communities, the treaty included promises of reserve land, financial payments, and farming tools. Treaty 1 continues to be important today as First Nations use it when discussing land rights and agreements with the government.
