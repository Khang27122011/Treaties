Introduction

Treaty 1 was signed on August 3, 1871, at Lower Fort Garry, near what is now Winnipeg, Manitoba. It was one of the first numbered treaties made after Canada became a country in 1867. The treaty was an agreement between the Canadian government and several First Nations groups, including the Anishinaabe (Ojibway) and the Swampy Cree. The main goal of the treaty was to establish peaceful relationships between the Indigenous peoples and the new Canadian government, while also allowing settlers to move into the area to farm and develop the land.

Signing the treaty was not a simple process. Leaders from both sides had to negotiate terms carefully. The First Nations wanted guarantees for their land, resources, and ways of life. The Canadian government wanted to encourage settlement and growth in the west. The treaty included promises such as payments to the First Nations, farming equipment to help transition to agriculture, and land reserves to ensure their communities could continue to live on and use their traditional territories.

Quick Facts About Treaty 1

  • Signed: August 3, 1871
  • Location: Lower Fort Garry, Manitoba
  • Nations involved: Anishinaabe (Ojibway) and Swampy Cree
  • One of the first numbered treaties in Canada

Signatories

The treaty was signed by Canadian government representatives including Adams George Archibald, the first Lieutenant Governor of Manitoba, and Wemyss M. Simpson, the Treaty Commissioner. They worked closely with First Nations leaders to agree on the terms. The Indigenous signatories represented the Anishinaabe (Ojibway) and Swampy Cree peoples, who had lived on this land for generations and had deep knowledge of the environment, including rivers, forests, and hunting grounds.

The negotiations focused on how the land would be shared and how the First Nations could maintain their traditions while the Canadian government expanded its settlement. The First Nations leaders wanted protections for hunting, fishing, and cultural practices. In return, they agreed to allow settlers to farm certain areas and to recognize Canadian authority over the land. This agreement set a precedent for future treaties between Canada and Indigenous peoples across the country.

Impact

Treaty 1 had a long-lasting impact on the land and people of Manitoba. It allowed the Canadian government to expand westward, build settlements, and develop farming communities. Roads, schools, and towns gradually appeared, changing the landscape and the way of life in the region. The treaty also opened the area to trade and economic development, connecting the west to other parts of Canada.

For the First Nations, Treaty 1 meant both challenges and protections. They received land reserves, financial support, and tools for farming, which helped communities adjust to changes brought by settlers. At the same time, they worked to preserve traditional practices like hunting, fishing, and gathering. The treaty remains important today, as it is a symbol of negotiation, understanding, and ongoing relationships between Indigenous peoples and the Canadian government. Many First Nations still refer to Treaty 1 when discussing land rights and community agreements.
