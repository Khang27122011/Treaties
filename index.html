Introduction

Background of Treaty 1

Treaty 1 was signed on August 3, 1871 at Lower Fort Garry near Winnipeg, Manitoba. It was one of the first numbered treaties made after Canada became a country in 1867. The treaty was an agreement between the Canadian government and several First Nations groups. The goal was to create peaceful relationships and allow settlers to move into the area. Today, Treaty 1 is an important part of Canadian history and Indigenous history.

Signatories

Key Leaders

The treaty was made between representatives of the Canadian government and leaders from several First Nations communities. The government representatives included Adams George Archibald and Treaty Commissioner Wemyss M. Simpson.

The First Nations involved included Anishinaabe (Ojibway) and Swampy Cree leaders. They agreed to share land with the Canadian government in exchange for promises such as payments, farming tools, and land reserves for their communities.

Impact

Long-Term Effects

Treaty 1 had a lasting impact on the people and land of Manitoba. It allowed the Canadian government to expand westward and bring more settlers into the region.

In return, First Nations were promised land, support, and the right to continue traditional activities such as hunting and fishing.
